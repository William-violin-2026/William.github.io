1. Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, learners will be able to:

  • Define the core concepts of the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML) and identify its primary author.
  • Explain the three cognitive assumptions underlying the theory (Dual Channels, Limited Capacity, Active Processing).
  • Distinguish between the three types of cognitive load (Intrinsic, Extraneous, Germane) and their relationship to video design.
  • Apply key multimedia learning principles (e.g., Coherence, Signaling, Redundancy) to evaluate and improve educational videos.
  • Design instructional video scripts or storyboards that align with how the human brain processes information.

2. Author of CTML

Richard E. Mayer

He is a Distinguished Professor of Psychology at the University of California, Santa Barbara (UCSB).

Mayer is a leading authority in educational psychology. His research focuses on applying the science of learning to education, specifically how to design multimedia instruction (videos, slides, online courses) to maximize learning outcomes.

His book, Multimedia Learning, is considered the seminal text in this field.

3. Introduction to CTML Model

The CTML model describes how the human mind processes information through auditory and visual systems. The model suggests that learning is a process of moving information from Sensory Memory to Working Memory, and finally integrating it into Long-Term Memory.

The Core Process:

  1. Selecting: The learner pays attention to relevant words and images from the presented material (video visuals, narration), bringing them into sensory memory.
  2. Organizing: The learner organizes selected words into a verbal model and selected images into a visual model within the working memory.
  3. Integrating: The learner connects the verbal and visual models with each other and with prior knowledge from long-term memory.

4. The Key Elements (Assumptions)

Mayer‚Äôs theory is built upon three fundamental assumptions from cognitive science:

1. Dual Channels Assumption

Humans possess separate channels for processing visual/pictorial material (eyes) and auditory/verbal material (ears). When watching a video, on-screen animations are processed via the visual channel, while narration is processed via the auditory channel.

2. Limited Capacity Assumption

Humans can only process a finite amount of information in each channel at any one time. If a video is too fast or information-dense, working memory becomes "overloaded," and learning fails.

3. Active Processing Assumption

Deep learning occurs when the learner engages in active cognitive processing (selecting, organizing, and integrating), rather than passively absorbing information.

6. Multimedia Learning Principles

Here are the most critical principles for Educational Video Production:

A. Reducing Extraneous Processing

  • Coherence Principle: Exclude extraneous words, pictures, and sounds.
    Application: Cut background music and decorative images.
  • Signaling Principle: Add cues to highlight organization.
    Application: Use arrows, highlights, or vocal emphasis.
  • Redundancy Principle: Graphics + Narration is better than Graphics + Narration + Text.
    Application: Avoid reading on-screen text word-for-word.
  • Spatial Contiguity Principle: Words and pictures should be near each other.
    Application: Place labels next to the object, not in a legend.

B. Managing Essential Processing

  • Segmenting Principle: User-paced segments are better than continuous units.
    Application: Break long videos into chapters or add "Click to Continue" buttons.
  • Pre-training Principle: Know names/characteristics of concepts beforehand.
    Application: Provide a glossary before the complex video animation begins.

C. Fostering Generative Processing

  • Multimedia Principle: Words + Pictures are better than Words alone.
    Application: Include diagrams/animations, not just a "talking head".
  • Personalization Principle: Conversational style is better than Formal style.
    Application: Use "I" and "You". Speak like a friend.
